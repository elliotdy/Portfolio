Twin Jet Airliner

A full aircraft conceptual and preliminary design project for a new narrow-body twinjet intended to compete with the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX families. The design process included concept screening, aerodynamic and propulsion trade studies, aircraft sizing, matching-chart analysis, and CAD development of the final configuration.

Aircraft Design Aerodynamics Performance Analysis Constraint Analysis CAD Modeling Trade Studies

Project Goal

The objective was to design an all-new narrow-body twinjet that could compete in the same market as the A320neo and 737 MAX, while targeting a 5–10% improvement in efficiency to justify a new program. Early in the project, the team framed the design around the key drivers of fuel burn per seat, reliability, development practicality, and long-term profitability. Rather than just creating a visually plausible aircraft, the project was treated as a system-level design problem where every major choice needed to support a competitive business and performance case.

How We Made Decisions

  • Benchmarked the aircraft against the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX families
  • Screened major concept directions before locking the final configuration
  • Used aerodynamic and technology trade studies to identify the strongest efficiency drivers
  • Selected a design point from constraint analysis instead of choosing geometry arbitrarily
  • Refined the aircraft through successive preliminary design reviews and CAD development

Final Configuration

The final aircraft layout was the result of multiple design phases rather than a single initial concept. As the project progressed, the aircraft was refined around a conventional narrow-body twinjet architecture because that layout best matched the target cruise speed, passenger count, operational practicality, and technology readiness required to challenge existing single-aisle commercial aircraft.

Fuel Burn as a Primary Driver

Fuel burn per seat became one of the most important success metrics in the project. By the later design stage, the project targeted a value better than the benchmark competitors, and the final comparison slide shows the design reaching 147 lbf per seat against an objective of 150, outperforming the listed A320neo, A321neo, and 737 MAX baselines. This made fuel efficiency one of the clearest indicators that the design direction was working and helped justify choices that favored aerodynamic efficiency and weight reduction.

Weight as a Practical Surrogate for Cost

In the earlier phases, the team had not yet completed a detailed cost estimate, so gross weight was used as a practical surrogate when comparing design competitiveness. A lighter aircraft generally supports lower structural demands, lower fuel burn, and a stronger path toward reduced operating cost. That made weight estimation more than a bookkeeping task — it directly influenced whether the design could realistically compete with current aircraft.

Design Logic

How the Aircraft Was Chosen

1. Constraint Analysis Set the Design Point

The matching chart was used to avoid choosing wing loading and thrust-to-weight ratio by intuition alone. The final design point shown here was selected at approximately Wo/S = 115 lb/ft² and To/Wo = 0.40, balancing takeoff, landing, climb, cruise, stall, and maneuver constraints in one location. This step was critical because it linked the aircraft geometry to mission and certification-style requirements instead of treating them as separate problems.

2. Airfoil and Wing Choices Came from Trade Studies

The wing design process started by defining target lift coefficient range, transonic cruise requirements, and aspect ratio goals. Several airfoil candidates were compared, including NACA 64A410, NACA 65-410, and NACA 63(2)-415, before the project moved toward a final configuration suited to the cruise and lift requirements. This was not just an aesthetic decision — the wing had to support cruise efficiency, lift capability, and acceptable stall behavior while remaining structurally realistic.

3. Aerodynamics Was Identified as a Major Efficiency Lever

In the conceptual phase, aerodynamic improvement was selected as one of the strongest directions to pursue because lift-to-drag ratio had a clear effect on weight and seat-mile fuel burn. The team found that increasing aspect ratio could improve efficiency while still staying within reasonable structural limits, making aerodynamic refinement much more valuable than chasing speculative architecture changes that offered weak or uncertain benefit.

Why the Design Stayed Conventional

One of the most important parts of the project was deciding what not to pursue. During conceptual design, the team examined hybrid-electric concepts, unducted engine ideas, and changes related to Oswald’s factor. Hybrid propulsion was rejected because current technology carried major weight, volume, and development penalties. Unducted propulsion was also rejected because the target cruise Mach number pushed beyond the practical benefits of that direction. Even Oswald-factor changes were set aside when they showed minimal payoff compared with the aerodynamic and technology changes being considered. The final aircraft therefore stayed with a more realistic commercial twinjet configuration that aligned with market demand and near-term feasibility.

Technology Was Chosen Where It Actually Mattered

Another major takeaway from the trade studies was that not all technology factors influenced the design equally. The project found that material technology factor had the strongest effect on gross weight and fuel burn, which made it a more useful design focus than changes with weaker system-level payoff. That helped keep the design grounded: instead of adding complexity for its own sake, the project emphasized the improvements that moved the aircraft most in the desired direction.

CAD Development

Major Aircraft Components

Fuselage

The fuselage sizing was driven by passenger accommodation, seat arrangement, aisle space, and total volume requirements. The design targeted a fineness ratio comparable to existing commercial aircraft so that the aircraft could carry the intended payload while remaining aerodynamically reasonable.

Wing + High-Lift Geometry

The wing was developed to support the aerodynamic goals identified in the trade studies, while the sectional and flap-related geometry helped connect the cruise design to takeoff and landing performance. This part of the project shows how the design moved from numerical requirements into physical aircraft geometry.

Empennage

The tail surfaces were sized to support the aircraft’s stability and control requirements. This stage tied the geometry back to the aircraft-level static stability work and helped ensure the final layout was not only efficient, but also controllable and balanced.

Landing Gear Layout

Landing gear geometry was developed as part of the full aircraft configuration, supporting ground stability, aircraft stance, and packaging within the final CAD layout. Even though it is a smaller subsystem compared with the wing or fuselage, it is an important example of how detailed design decisions had to fit within the larger aircraft architecture.

Final Three-View Development

These views show the aircraft as a complete integrated system. By this point, the project had already passed through concept screening, performance sizing, aerodynamic trade studies, and subsystem geometry development. The final three-view is valuable because it represents the moment when all of those independent design choices had to coexist in one coherent aircraft.

What This Project Demonstrates

This project demonstrates system-level aircraft design thinking: benchmarking against real competitors, screening advanced concepts, identifying meaningful performance drivers, selecting a design point from constraints, and carrying the design through into physical geometry and CAD. The strongest part of the project is not any single figure or model, but the fact that the final configuration was supported by a chain of engineering decisions.
